# The Complete Pre-Buy Inspection Checklist

The pre-buy inspection is your last, best chance to catch problems before money changes hands. A thorough inspection by a qualified mechanic can save you tens of thousands of dollars in unexpected repairs. Use this checklist to understand what should be examined, what questions to ask, and what red flags indicate deeper issues.

## Before Scheduling the Inspection

**Documentation Review** (you can do this yourself):
- [ ] Request all maintenance logs for the past 10 years (5 minimum)
- [ ] Verify annual inspection compliance—no gaps allowed
- [ ] Review all Service Bulletins and Airworthiness Directives (ADs)
- [ ] Confirm all ADs have been complied with and documented
- [ ] Check engine reserve—time since major overhaul (SMOH/TSMOH)
- [ ] Look for repetitive maintenance items
- [ ] Request damage history or any insurance claims
- [ ] Verify the aircraft registration is current and in the seller's name
- [ ] Check the logbook signatures are legible and dated correctly

## Airframe Inspection

**Exterior Fuselage**:
- [ ] Walk around entire aircraft looking for dents, crumpling, or waviness
- [ ] Check paint for crazing or signs of previous repainting (indicates damage)
- [ ] Inspect all access panels for corrosion around edges
- [ ] Look for loose rivets or evidence of skin repair
- [ ] Check windscreen and side windows for cracks
- [ ] Inspect doors and windows for proper seal and operation

**Wings and Flight Surfaces**:
- [ ] Walk wingspan, looking for waviness or damage
- [ ] Check aileron and flap operation—should be smooth with no binding
- [ ] Inspect wing struts (if applicable) for cracks or corrosion
- [ ] Verify control surfaces move freely and return to neutral
- [ ] Look for signs of glue separation on composite aircraft
- [ ] Check leading edge for dents or bird strikes
- [ ] Examine fuel tank sight glasses for contamination or leaking

**Tail Section**:
- [ ] Inspect stabilator and elevator for corrosion
- [ ] Verify control surface movement is smooth and full
- [ ] Check trim tab operation and security
- [ ] Inspect fuselage near tail for stress cracks or waviness
- [ ] Look for evidence of tail strike or prop strike damage

**Landing Gear**:
- [ ] Check tire condition, wear pattern, and proper inflation
- [ ] Inspect brake pads for thickness and uneven wear
- [ ] Look for brake fluid leaks
- [ ] Verify landing light operation
- [ ] Check gear doors for dents and proper sealing
- [ ] For retractable gear: test full up/down cycle and warning light function

**Interior**:
- [ ] Check seat condition and attachment
- [ ] Verify seatbelt and harness operation and condition
- [ ] Inspect cabin walls and headliner for water damage or stains
- [ ] Check carpeting for evidence of moisture
- [ ] Look for corrosion around door seals
- [ ] Verify door operation—should latch solidly and seal well

## Engine Inspection

This is where your qualified A&P mechanic earns their fee:

**Visual Inspection**:
- [ ] Check engine exterior for oil seepage around gaskets and seals
- [ ] Inspect magneto and alternator for condition and security
- [ ] Check fuel injectors for leaking or corrosion
- [ ] Verify spark plug wires are properly seated
- [ ] Inspect oil dip stick—verify actual oil level matches book recommendation
- [ ] Check engine-driven vacuum pump condition and output
- [ ] Verify propeller spinner and blade condition

**Compression Test**:
- [ ] Verify all cylinders achieve proper compression (typically 60-70 psi minimum)
- [ ] No single cylinder should be more than 10 psi below the others
- [ ] Record all readings in writing

**Borescope Inspection**:
- [ ] Visual inspection of intake and exhaust valve condition
- [ ] Check for carbon buildup, scoring, or pitting
- [ ] Look for signs of oil burning or valve damage
- [ ] Inspect piston crowns for damage
- [ ] Critical—can reveal wear that low compressions suggest

**Oil Analysis** (optional but recommended):
- [ ] Request mechanic perform oil analysis if engine has decent time
- [ ] Borescope and compressions are usually sufficient for pre-buy

## Systems and Components

**Fuel System**:
- [ ] Verify fuel strainers are clear and no visible contamination
- [ ] Check fuel lines for deterioration or cracking
- [ ] Inspect fuel tank access panels for corrosion
- [ ] Verify fuel pump operation (listen for hum)
- [ ] Check fuel selector valve operation—should click solidly into each position
- [ ] Look for signs of fuel smell (leaking seals) around fuel cap

**Electrical System**:
- [ ] Test battery voltage at rest (should be 24+ volts for 24V system)
- [ ] Verify alternator output while running
- [ ] Check all avionics power and operation
- [ ] Test lights—nav, landing, strobe, cabin lights
- [ ] Verify radio and transponder transmit and receive

**Flight Controls**:
- [ ] Manually move each control surface through full range
- [ ] Listen for unusual noises or grinding
- [ ] Verify trim operation (pitch and roll if equipped)
- [ ] Check for excessive play in control columns or yokes
- [ ] Confirm ailerons respond smoothly

**Instruments and Avionics**:
- [ ] Verify all six primary flight instruments operate
- [ ] Check artificial horizon cage operation
- [ ] Test airspeed indicator—needle should move smoothly
- [ ] Verify altimeter setting mechanism and operation
- [ ] Check heading indicator and compass
- [ ] Test all avionics against equipment list in POH
- [ ] Verify glass cockpit systems if installed (software version, database currency)

**Environmental Systems**:
- [ ] Test cabin heat and ventilation
- [ ] Verify oxygen system if installed (check pressure, flow)
- [ ] Check defroster operation
- [ ] Inspect air filters for condition

## Major Red Flags—Stop and Reconsider

If your mechanic finds any of these during the pre-buy, seriously reconsider the purchase:

- **Corrosion**: Significant aluminum corrosion suggests poor maintenance history
- **Cracked engine case**: Usually uneconomical to repair
- **Prop damage not documented**: Undisclosed prop strikes indicate hidden damage
- **Gaps in annual inspections**: Aircraft was legally unairworthy during gaps
- **Incomplete AD compliance**: Can't legally fly until corrected
- **Flight control play or grinding**: Dangerous—requires immediate repair
- **Soft/wet spots in fuselage**: Indicates internal corrosion and structural compromise
- **Compression below 50 psi on any cylinder**: Suggests imminent overhaul
- **Electrical system failures**: Expensive to troubleshoot and repair

## After the Inspection

- [ ] Get a written inspection report detailing all findings
- [ ] Request cost estimates for any deferred maintenance items
- [ ] Ask mechanic's honest assessment: "Would you fly this aircraft tomorrow?"
- [ ] Factor repair costs into your offer price
- [ ] Ask about airworthiness concerns that must be corrected before flight

## Final Thoughts

A thorough pre-buy inspection typically costs $1,500-$3,000 but can save $20,000+ in unexpected repairs. Choose a mechanic who specializes in your aircraft type and has a good reputation in the local flying community. You can't un-buy an aircraft, but you can walk away from one—and sometimes walking away is the smartest decision you'll make.

Fly safe, and buy right.
